The Civil Rights Movement

February 12, 1909: NAACP was Created

1942: CORE was Founded

May 17, 1954: Brown v. Board of Education

Brown v. Board of Education is one of the most significant Supreme Court cases in American history, as it fundamentally transformed the U.S. education system and civil rights law. The case was decided on May 17, 1954, and its ruling declared that racial segregation in public schools was unconstitutional.

December 1, 1955: Rosa Parks and the Montgomery Bus Boycott

In 1955, Rosa Parks was arrested in Montgomery, Alabama for refusing to give up her seat to a white man on a segregated bus. Her arrest sparked the Montgomery Bus Boycott, led by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., where African-Americans refused to ride city buses to protest segregation. The boycott lasted 381 days and successfully pressured the city to desegregate its buses. In 1956, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that bus segregation was unconstitutional.

April 1960: SNCC was Establish

January 10, 1957: SCLC was Launched

August 28, 1963: March on Washington and "I Have a Dream" Speech

The March on Washington was a landmark event where over 250,000 people gathered to demand racial equality and economic justice, and Martin Luther King Jr. delivered his iconic "I Have a Dream" speech, calling for a future of racial harmony, justice, and equality for all Americans.

July 2, 1964: Civil Rights Act of 1964

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 was a landmark law that banned discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. It ended segregation in public places, such as schools, workplaces, and restaurants, and ensured equal access to voting, education, and employment. This major legislation played a key role in advancing the Civil Rights Movement and promoting equality in the United States.

August 6, 1965: Voting Rights Act of 1965

The Voting Rights Act of 1965 was a landmark law designed to eliminate racial discrimination in voting, particularly in the South. It banned discriminatory practices like literacy tests and poll taxes, which had been used to disenfranchise African Americans. This legislation played a vital role in significantly increasing African American voter participation and advancing civil rights, marking a crucial victory in the fight for equality in the United States.

April 4, 1968: Assassination of Martin Luther King Jr.

The assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. in 1968 galvanized the Civil Rights Movement, drawing greater national attention to racial inequality. It intensified activism and led to the passage of the Civil Rights Act of 1968, which addressed housing discrimination. His death also sparked widespread protests and a renewed commitment to nonviolent resistance, inspiring continued efforts for equality and keeping the movement's momentum strong.
